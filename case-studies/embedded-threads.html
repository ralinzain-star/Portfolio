Embedded Threads: Bringing BI to Where Decisions Actually Happen
Designing a conversational BI surface that lives inside Notion sidebars, mobile bottom-sheets, and 393px phone screens, without losing its voice along the way.

Project Overview

Wren AI is a conversational BI product. What customers kept asking for wasn't another tab to open, it was a way to ask their data questions where they already were: a Notion sidebar on a laptop, a bottom-sheet modal on their phone, a panel in a Tableau dashboard between meetings. Embedded Threads is the surface I designed to live in those places, from a 1024px desktop split to a 393px phone screen, without losing what makes Wren AI itself.

My Role

Sole designer. I owned the embedded experience end to end, how it gets configured, how it adapts across screen sizes, what it says when something breaks, and how it behaves once it's living inside someone else's product.

Company & Timeframe

Wren AI · Feb – March 2026 · 2 weeks

01. BI Doesn't Live on a Dashboard Anymore. It Lives Where the Decision Is.

A BI tool that only opens in its own tab is a tool people forget. The customers who actually used Wren AI were the ones who could pull it up while they were already mid-task, reading a Notion doc on the train, checking an internal dashboard from their phone in a meeting, glancing at a Tableau panel between slides. Embedding wasn't a nice-to-have. It was the difference between a tool people remember and a tool they don't.

Surfaces · Notion · Mobile dashboards · Tableau Screen sizes · 393 → 1024px+ Release · Wren 2.0 · March 2026 Constraint · 2-week ship window

The behavior I was designing for

The clearest signal was watching how people actually asked their data questions. They weren't sitting at their desks. They were on phones during commutes, on tablets in meeting rooms, on laptops with three other tabs already open. The embed had to be a surface they could glance at, ask once, and act on, without ever feeling like they'd left the host product to do it.

The design bet

Treat Embedded Threads as its own surface, not a smaller version of the main app. That meant designing from the constraints inward: a 393px phone width, a bottom-sheet that animates up over content, a sidebar that's never wider than 30% of the host page. If it worked at 393, it would work everywhere else. If it only worked at 1024, it didn't matter.

The framing question, where will the user be when they ask this? Not what data model they need, not what permissions they should have, but physically where, on what screen, with how much attention. Answering that first reordered every decision after.

02. Two Modes, Built Around How People Actually Embed

The tempting move was one "smart" mode that handled every case. The disciplined move was to refuse. Two clear surfaces, each tied to a real behavior, drop into a doc, or sign in and see your own history.

Mode 01 · Public Public Embed A shared chat. Anyone landing on the page sees the same threads, useful for Notion pages, public documentation, marketing sites.
Best for Notion sidebars, Tableau panels, public docs
What the user sees One shared thread. Everyone reads the same conversation.
Setup Toggle on. Copy. Paste. Under a minute.
Default Off. The embed never ships in a state that could leak data accidentally.
Mode 02 · Identity Verification Identity Verification Embed A personal chat. The host product hands off who the user is when the embed loads, so each person sees only their own history.
Best for Mobile dashboards, internal SaaS, anything with a login
What the user sees Their own threads, their own history. Nobody else's.
Setup A bit more involved, the host product passes the user's identity in.
Why split A shared embed in a Notion doc and a personal embed in a logged-in mobile dashboard are two different products. Pretending they're the same makes both worse.

Naming the trade-off was the work. An afternoon went into deciding not to merge the two. Once Public and Identity each had a name and a default, every later question, what shows when nobody's logged in, when threads should refresh, what the empty state says, had an obvious answer. Ambiguity in the model is the cost you pay later in the UI.

03. Watched the Neighbors First, Then Decided What Not to Copy

Embedded analytics isn't new. Before I opened Figma I spent a week with Metabase, Tableau, and Power BI, not to copy any of them, but to know where the industry converged and where I could still simplify.

Open-source benchmark Metabase What I saw: the lowest-friction path from "I want this in my doc" to "it's running." Cleanest first-run feel in the category. What I took: that drop-in feeling. Customers shouldn't have to read a week of docs to try a v1.
Enterprise gold standard Tableau What I saw: every setting exposed, every behavior programmable. Powerful, but heavy. What I skipped: the programmable surface. Most of our customers want to drop the embed into a Notion page or a mobile dashboard, not write code against an SDK.
Microsoft ecosystem Power BI What I saw: identity and data permissions handled as separate concerns. Conceptually elegant. What I parked for v2: that two-layer split. For v1 customers, one layer is enough, the second is design overhead, not value.

The principle I walked away with, do what the industry agrees on, and don't do what the industry over-does. "Copy-paste a snippet and you're running" became the bar for v1, and that one sentence shaped every embed setup screen that followed.

04. Five Design Calls Running in Parallel

Picking the mode was step one. Underneath sat five design problems that had to land at the same time, usage limits, activity records, brand customization, what to hide from the main app, and how to talk when something breaks. Each had its own edge cases, and each had to feel like a local resident inside someone else's product.

01 · Usage limits
Don't let the embed burn a month of budget overnight

Every question costs the customer something. Without a cap, a single bug in a client's page could drain their month in an afternoon. I set a default ceiling per project, auto-resetting on the first, and made the over-cap state read-only instead of an error: the thread stays visible, just nobody can ask. The embed never turns blank in front of someone's customer.

Scope ByProject Per project, not per user. The project is what gets billed.
Default cap 20credits Enough for a demo to feel real, not enough to do damage.
Reset Monthly First of the month, automatic.
Over-cap Read-only Thread still visible, asking disabled. Never blank.
02 · Activity log
Who asked what, when, how much

The embed runs outside the main app, but a Project Owner still needs to answer the obvious question, who asked what, when, costing how much, for compliance, for debugging, for billing. I scoped the log to the actions that actually matter to a project owner, just enough to be useful, not so much that it becomes noise.

  • Created a thread, with the first question that opened it
  • Asked a follow-up, who continued which conversation
  • Deleted a thread, who and when
  • Changed embed settings, who toggled what (mode, branding, limits)
  • Rotated the access key, with a deliberate grace period so live sessions don't drop

One small decision I'm glad I held. The thread titles in the log are not clickable for embedded threads, with a tooltip explaining why. Clicking through would open the main app and expose a surface the embed was specifically designed to keep hidden. The log is a record, not a back door.

03 · Brand customization
Just enough to feel like a local resident, no more

The embed has to look like it belongs in the host product, but when something goes wrong, the user has to be able to recognize it as Wren AI. I kept the customizable surface to four things: logo, chat icon, primary color, display name. Everything else inherits the defaults. Every extra "yes, you can change this" is a setup task you've added to the customer's afternoon, keeping the surface small was a courtesy, not a limitation.

  • Company logo, shown at the iframe header
  • Chat icon, the AI avatar in each response
  • Theme color, primary accent, follows host product
  • Display name, how the embed refers to itself in UI copy
  • Responsive layout, holds up from a Notion sidebar to a phone bottom-sheet to a full-page iframe

RWD playground, drag the corner and watch it collapse

The same embed has to live inside a Notion sidebar (~393px), a phone bottom-sheet (~768px), and a full-page iframe (~1024px+). I designed an explicit collapse priority, the thread list goes first, then the follow-up chips, then the chart shrinks. Mobile-first isn't a smaller desktop. It's the opposite, get 393 right first, and the wider sizes earn their extra space.

04 · Hiding the workshop
So it reads as a conversation, not a tool

The main Wren AI app exposes a lot, suggestion chips, thinking steps, candidate models, query provenance. Useful for power users, noise the moment the embed lands in someone's Notion page. In the embed I hid most of it, and kept exactly one thing the others didn't get, suggested follow-ups. They're the cheapest way to keep a user moving after an answer, and they read as conversational, not technical. Hide what's about how the answer was made, keep what's about what to ask next.

05 · How the embed talks when it breaks
Three severities, one rule, never blank

The embed will fail, tokens expire, models drift, quotas run out, users aren't always logged in. The rule I held to: the embedded surface never goes blank, and the user always knows whose problem this is. I grouped failures into three severities, each with its own UI treatment, so the wording, icon, and CTA could follow from one decision instead of fifteen.

  • Full-page block, the embed can't run at all. Not logged in, not configured, the service is down. Honest, plain, says exactly what's missing and who can fix it.
  • Read-only, the embed can be looked at but not used. Quota's gone, model can't parse. The thread stays, the input is locked, the explanation is one line.
  • In-thread notice, this question failed but the conversation continues. No data matched, query couldn't run. A small message in the bubble, then the user moves on.

The hardest part wasn't the categorization, it was the wording. "Something went wrong" doesn't pass when the embed is sitting in someone else's product, in front of someone else's user. Every line had to say what happened, what they can do, what they shouldn't try. Speaking for Wren AI on a screen it doesn't own meant every word counted.

05. One Embed, Wherever It's Hosted

Wren AI ships in two flavors, a managed cloud version and a self-hosted version customers run themselves. The embed had to behave the same in both. Most of the design work in this section wasn't building two surfaces, it was making the differences underneath invisible to the person actually using the embed.

What stays the same on both

Same setup screen. Same error messages. Same responsive behavior, including the 393px collapse. A user reading the embed shouldn't be able to tell whether the customer is on cloud or self-hosted, and a customer evaluating both shouldn't have to learn two different products.

What's deliberately different

Self-hosted gates this feature behind the higher license tier, so the setup screen needed a graceful "not available on your plan" state, not hidden (confusing) but visible-and-locked, with a clear explanation. Same activity is visible in just one place per environment, not duplicated across two views, that took longer to get right than expected.

The invisible alignment. Consistent user experience isn't about making the back end identical, it's about deciding which differences to hide and which to name out loud. Hide the wrong one and you look dishonest. Name the wrong one and you look messy. Where to draw that line was one of the longer judgment calls of this project.
