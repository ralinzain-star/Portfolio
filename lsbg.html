AI Bicycle Lane Maintenance Tool
Collecting and analyzing Hamburg citizen reports to improve cycling infrastructure

Quick Product Intro:

BumpHunter is an AI-powered tool for Landesbetrieb Straßen, Brücken und Gewässer (LSBG) that collects and analyzes citizen feedback on bicycle lane conditions and maintenance needs. It leverages Google Maps, Strava cycling activity data, and city weather information to provide automated visualizations—enabling engineers to detect issues, prioritize repairs, and enhance cycling infrastructure with greater precision.

My Role:

As the Interaction Designer, I planned two preliminary research studies covering both the citizen side and the bicycle engineer side, and facilitated over 20 cross-cultural interviews. Throughout development, I conducted rolling studies to ensure insights were translated into concrete, effective design decisions, and created high-fidelity prototypes to validate functionality and usability. I worked closely with team members from Mexico, India, Turkey, Germany, and Russia, aligning stakeholders on interaction patterns to ensure the final experience met the cultural and environmental needs of the city of Hamburg.

01. Where We Started

Project Background:

Inspection, maintenance, and planning of bicycle lanes require coordination across multiple government departments and inspection agencies. Although citizens can report issues, many still come through traditional channels like email, resulting in fragmented and hard-to-use information. Drone-based solutions can help in some situations, but their effectiveness drops significantly during winter.

Our Goal:

Develop a lightweight solution that delivers timely, organized updates on bicycle lane conditions — allowing engineers to cut down on on-site inspections, work more efficiently with other departments, and target repairs where they're needed most, even in challenging winter conditions.

02. Investigate What Was Broken

Preliminary Research:

We conducted research combining secondary sources and primary data collection to understand the challenges and opportunities in bicycle lane planning and inspection.

Secondary research referenced literature on European bicycle lane infrastructure, including planning processes and inspection checkpoints (e.g., snow-related maintenance). Internal system operation documents from LSBG were also reviewed.

Primary research involved interviews with bicycle lane experts, engineers, and cycling enthusiasts, as well as focus groups and on-site observations.

Here are some key quotes from our interviews:

"The success of the Netherlands in creating a comprehensive network of dedicated cycle paths." — Professor Chris Brunlett

"What is currently lacking is the feedback from citizens regarding their riding experiences on bicycle paths." — LSBG Bike Lane Engineer

"If my contributions truly help improve my neighborhood, I would be more willing to report." — Citizen

Key Insights:

Our reports showed that engineers lack detailed information on which road segments matter most to citizens and the feedback from their riding experiences. At the same time, citizens are eager for their reports to be acknowledged by the government, which could drive a more proactive approach to inspections.

Persona:

Our research revealed 2 key roles shaping the bicycle lane planning and inspection process: the Citizen and the Engineer.

Bicycle Lane / Road Engineer Resident / Cyclist
Key Focus Maintenance and inspection efficiency Reporting road conditions, improving neighborhoods
Key Features Technical inspection, data analysis Submitting reports, tracking responses
Needs Clear citizen feedback on road quality and priority segments Assurance that feedback is heard and acted on

While their perspectives differ — from lived riding experiences to technical maintenance priorities — both face similar challenges, including insufficient communication, incomplete data, and the absence of an effective channel that enables their needs and perspectives to be exchanged in both directions and meaningfully influence planning decisions.

Tips: How to Solve Language Barrier with AI Tools

  • Transcribe fast — Used Descript to turn German interviews into text.
  • Summarize smart — Let Hypotenuse pull out key points with prompt-based summaries.
  • Translate quickly — Ran outputs through online tools for English context.
  • Visualize instantly — Used QoQo.ai in Figma to map personas & journeys.
03. Our Solution

Service Blueprint:

Engineers often struggled with bicycle lane maintenance: they didn't know which areas to fix first, and citizen reports were scattered and inconsistent. To close this gap, we designed a two-way loop. Engineers could spot damage and traffic flow on maps, then send specific "missions" to riders. Citizens responded with locations, photos, and riding feedback, turning fragmented reports into clear, actionable data. What was once guesswork became a data-driven system for timely inspections and smarter maintenance.

Next, let's take a closer look at how this tool enables engineers to assess priority areas for bicycle lane maintenance through maps, and request specific bicycle lane information from citizens based on their needs:

Two Styles of Information Visualization:

Map View:

Map View enables engineers to quickly pinpoint priority areas for bicycle lane maintenance by visualizing damage severity, report density, and traffic levels. Color-coded markers make it easy to distinguish between extremely dangerous, dangerous, and minor issues, while traffic overlays reveal usage intensity. This combination helps engineers decide not just where intervention is most urgent, but also where repairs will have the greatest impact.

[ Map View screen recording ]

Report View:

Report View transforms citizen feedback into structured, actionable data, presenting an at-a-glance summary of bicycle lane conditions by district. For example, in Bergstedt, the Uncomfortable rate is 60% and the Dangerous rate is 70%, with key issues including low road traction, numerous potholes, and hazardous obstacles like tree branches. The view also shows report counts and traffic demand distribution (High 30.5%, Medium 4.5%, Low 65%), enabling engineers to quickly gauge both usage and risk levels.

[ Report View screen recording ]

Get Road Usage Information from Citizens:

Send Mission:

When engineers need more context to assess a specific area, they can use Send Mission to directly engage local residents. This feature sends targeted requests to citizens in the program, asking them to capture photos, record observations, and provide detailed feedback about the road segment in question. By gathering on-the-ground insights from actual users, engineers can validate existing data, fill in missing details, and make more accurate maintenance or planning decisions.

[ Send Mission screen recording ]

Citizen-Assisted Reporting of Bicycle Routes:

Capture cycling experience with simple clicks:

Hamburg residents can play an active role in improving local cycling infrastructure by sharing real-world road usage feedback via the Road Usage Reporting Website. Each submission helps engineers better understand on-the-ground conditions and prioritize repairs that matter most to the community.

How it works:

  1. Enter the address of the damaged area
  2. Adjust the map pin for accuracy
  3. Upload photos of the issue
  4. Indicate the level of danger and discomfort

With just a few steps, residents can transform their daily riding experiences into actionable insights, directly influencing maintenance priorities and ensuring safer, more comfortable bike lanes.

04. What's the Cool Parts in This Project?
