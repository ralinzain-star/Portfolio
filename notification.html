Notification Framework
Turning scattered alerts into a unified, review-ready notification framework.

Project Overview:

The Notification Framework is a governance system for how notifications are created, classified, and delivered across product teams. It gives designers and PMs a consistent way to define intent, timing, and priority before messages reach users.

The work introduced structured rules, expanded notification categories, and defined frequency and priority models. I also helped design a supporting tool that turns the framework into a reviewable workflow for planning and validation.

My Role:

As the interaction designer, I audited and classified existing notifications, co-developed the decision framework, and translated it into practical review tools. I aligned cross-functional teams on shared standards, helped define the supporting classifier workflow, and documented the system so it could scale beyond a single feature team.

Company & Timeframe:

Google Pixel · 2025

0. Behind the Scenes

Every feature was shipping notifications in isolation, creating duplicates, gaps, and unclear ownership across the product.

How might we build a framework that helps teams decide what to send, when, and why?

01. Where We Started

Project Background:

Notifications across features lived in separate files and were reviewed in isolation. Without a shared view, teams struggled to tell whether similar messages already existed, how they were triggered, or whether they conflicted with other parts of the product.

Our Goal:

We set out to build a shared notification framework that:

  • Aligns designers and PMs on what to send, when, and why.
  • Reduces redundancy while preserving important alerts.
  • Turns fragmented guidelines into a single source of truth for planning and design.
02. Our Process

To make the framework usable beyond a single review cycle, we structured the work in three stages: audit the current state, define the classification logic, and pressure-test the system with cross-functional teams.

Step 1 — Notification Audit

We started by auditing six core features, collecting all existing notifications into a single database. Each one was mapped by trigger, urgency, and frequency, then translated into flowcharts to reconstruct the end-to-end user journey.

Step 2 — Classification & Rules

From the audit, we designed a classification tree that asked simple but systematic questions: Is this user-initiated? Is it a device health warning? Is it contextual? Each answer led to a category, a frequency type, and an importance level.

Step 3 — Cross-Functional Alignment

We partnered closely with PMs and engineers, running design reviews and scenario testing to pressure-test the framework against real use cases. These sessions revealed where redundancy was most painful and where consistency mattered most — shaping the framework into something practical, not just theoretical.

03. What We Built

Categorization & Usage Guidelines

Building on Android guidelines, we defined 8 categories (e.g., Critical Device Health Warning, Security & Privacy, Background Actions, Contextual Insights). This gave teams a vocabulary to consistently describe what kind of message they were sending.

Frequency Type Guidelines

We outlined 4 main delivery types: One-time, Reappearing, Regular Delivery, and Ongoing. Each had clear rules on urgency, when it should disappear, and how often it could reappear. This gave teams explicit guardrails against over-notification and alert fatigue.

Ranking & Priority Guidelines

Borrowing from Android's urgency heuristics, we mapped notifications to importance levels (Urgent, High, Medium, Low). This ensured both consistency in tone and alignment with system behaviors (sound, vibration, heads-up, etc.).

Notification Helper Tool

A static framework alone wasn't enough — we needed a way for teams to actually use it in daily workflows. To solve this, I designed the Notification Classifier Bot Prompt, a structured system that turned design rules into actionable outputs across different input types (PRDs, mocks, screenshots, flowcharts).

04. How I Designed the Prompt
1

Defining the Role & Modes

I set a clear role for the model ("Notification Classifier Bot") and introduced input-driven mode switching. Depending on whether the input was text, planning documents, or flow diagrams, the bot automatically applied the right logic. This reduced ambiguity and ensured each context was handled appropriately.

2

Standardized Review Table

To make outputs directly usable, I created a fixed review table with fields: Feature, Trigger, Category, Frequency, Importance, Priority, Disappear Conditions, Layouts/Actions, and Display. Any inferred data had to be flagged as (Gemini's Addition), making results traceable, auditable, and review-ready.

3

Anchoring to Core Documents

The bot was anchored to three authoritative sources — Categories & Usage, Frequency Types, and the Decision Tree. Each classification referenced these definitions and included a summary, ensuring consistency with the framework instead of language-based guesswork.

4

Handling Complex Inputs

I wrote strict rules for flowchart reviews, where noise is common. For flowchart inputs, only yellow cards were processed, one per row, while non-notification elements were ignored. Even identical texts with different triggers became separate entries, keeping discussions strictly focused on notifications.

5

Explainable Rules & Feedback Loop

Importance and priority were derived through explainable rules (Category → Frequency → System Behavior). Subjective scoring was replaced with logic teams could debate. I also embedded a feedback loop: if a classification was challenged, the bot suggested alternatives, recalculated values, and checked against Notification Overview for duplicates.

05. Impact

Build the Foundation

  • Goal: Establish a unified notification framework to give the team a consistent way to classify and govern notifications.
  • Impact: Grounding the work in three core documents — Categories, Frequency Types, and Decision Tree — gave the team a shared language. Review discussions shifted from individual interpretation to a more consistent decision process.

Ensure Consistency

  • Goal: Prevent users from receiving duplicate or conflicting messages, improving overall experience consistency.
  • Impact: Structured rules made de-duplication part of the workflow. The Classifier Bot checked triggers for overlap and flagged inferred fields, reducing both omissions and review friction.

Enable Better Work

  • Goal: Help designers and PMs make clearer decisions earlier in the process.
  • Impact: The prompt introduced MVP/long-term splits, explainable importance rules, and alternative suggestions, helping product planning move earlier and with clearer rationale.

In the end, the framework turned a scattered notification problem into a repeatable process. It gave the team a shared foundation for design reviews, trade-off discussions, and clearer accountability across features.
